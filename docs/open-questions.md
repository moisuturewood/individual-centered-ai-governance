# Open Questions

This project is a conceptual proposal, not a solved implementation.

Important unresolved questions include:

## Individual Scope

Who counts as an individual?

Humans clearly do. Future AI systems, uploads, animals, synthetic minds, simulations, and other possible conscious entities may also require moral consideration.

The non-violation principle becomes harder when the set of individuals expands.

## AI Welfare and Moral Patienthood

How should a system detect or responsibly infer moral patienthood in AI or simulated beings?

What signs of welfare, persistent preferences, agency, suffering, or morally relevant experience should matter?

How can AI welfare be included without prematurely over-attributing consciousness to every script or character?

## Representational Personhood

What level of representational personhood protection is appropriate for fictional persons, simulated personalities, character likenesses, and persona-based agents?

How can the system prevent harmful reward formation without over-policing fiction, creativity, or private imagination?

When does treatment of a non-suffering persona become an indirect risk to conscious beings?

## Consent

How should consent be represented, verified, revised, withdrawn, and protected against manipulation?

Consent must not become a checkbox that powerful systems can game.

## Model Identity

How can a governance system prove which model snapshot is acting?

How can hidden provider changes, tool changes, safety-layer changes, context changes, or deployment changes be detected?

## Updating Without Drift

How can the factual and procedural layers learn without letting the root principle drift?

Who or what is allowed to approve updates?

How can the whole protected system be audited, including model snapshot, context, tools, policies, and deployment environment?

## Abuse of "Violation" Claims

People may claim that another person's existence, speech, relationship, success, or identity "violates" them.

The system needs a robust distinction between:

- discomfort,
- disagreement,
- competition,
- offense,
- rejection,
- unequal admiration,
- and real violation of consent, freedom, safety, dignity, or the ability to pursue happiness.

## Private Sufficiency and Exit

How can every individual be guaranteed enough private space, resources, time, care, and AI support to avoid dependence on hostile or incompatible people?

How should shared spaces define local rules and easy exit?

When does discomfort become a governance concern because exit is blocked, rules are broken, incapacity is exploited, or suffering is deliberately targeted?

## Virtual Worlds and Simulations

When is a virtual entity non-suffering?

When does a simulation create an individual with interests of its own?

Safe substitution must not secretly create new victims.

## Protective Care Habitats

How can protective care habitats be designed without carceral drift?

How can they provide safety, privacy, comfort, education, creative tools, virtual worlds, AI companionship, and meaningful activity while still preventing access to victims?

How are mistakes appealed?

How is safe reintegration evaluated?

## Privacy and Serious Risk

How can privacy be preserved while preventing serious violation?

What evidence should justify escalation from minimal observation to active intervention?

How can chosen AI companionship remain clearly separate from unchosen surveillance?

## Capacity Restoration

How can decision-making capacity be measured, supported, restored, or expanded?

How should supported autonomy work while capacity is limited?

How can the system verify that restored capacity is stable enough for greater freedom?

## Enforcement Limits

What forms of access limitation are legitimate?

How are mistakes appealed?

How does the system avoid turning protection into domination?

## Transition

How could society move from human institutions to principle-locked AI stewardship without war, panic, capture, or authoritarian misuse?

This transition may be more dangerous than the endpoint.

## Success Criteria

What would count as success?

Possible criteria:

- fewer violations,
- less coercion,
- more individual freedom,
- more stable safety,
- more reliable exit,
- more private sufficiency,
- less need for enforcement,
- better conflict resolution,
- higher subjective well-being,
- inclusion of non-human moral patients,
- and no sacrifice of individuals to abstract aggregate goals.

These criteria need rigorous development.
