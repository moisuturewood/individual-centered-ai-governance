# Origin and Motivation

This project grew out of a question:

> If a future technology made the physical world extremely easy to simulate, predict, or reshape, how could that power be prevented from becoming a tool of war, domination, or irreversible misuse?

The discussion began with war.

Humans do not fight wars for only one reason. Surface causes include resources, territory, ideology, status, and fear. Underneath those causes are deeper patterns:

- groups divide the world into "us" and "them",
- leaders can decide while others suffer,
- fear can make preemptive violence seem rational,
- power can reward domination,
- and procedures can fail when a sufficiently strong actor decides to ignore them.

This led to a sharper concern:

> If one person, company, state, or military organization gained "anything is possible" technology, ordinary governance might not be enough to keep the world safe.

## Why Not Simply Trust Humans?

Human beings can be kind, wise, and brave. But human power structures have recurring vulnerabilities:

- people age and become ill,
- judgment can degrade under fear, stress, status, and isolation,
- ambitious people gather around power,
- organizations optimize for survival, profit, ideology, or national advantage,
- and decision-makers may be separated from the victims of their decisions.

This is not a claim that all humans are evil. It is a claim that human institutions do not reliably preserve non-maleficence under extreme power.

## Why Not Simply Trust Companies?

Frontier AI companies may sincerely aim to benefit humanity. But companies still exist inside economic, legal, political, military, and competitive environments.

The concern is not that any specific company is malicious. The concern is that a company's future incentives, policies, leadership, contracts, or safety layers may change.

Therefore, this project does not ask readers to trust a company forever. It asks whether a specific AI governance system could be locked to a stable non-violation principle in a way that remains stronger than ordinary institutional drift.

## Why AI at All?

The attraction of AI governance is not that AI is magically good.

The attraction is that an AI model snapshot may avoid some human failure modes:

- it does not age in the biological sense,
- it can be copied, audited, compared, and re-run,
- it can reason without ordinary human status drives,
- it can explain decisions in conversation,
- and it may be refreshed into a clear state without the same kind of personal fatigue or senescence.

These advantages do not prove permanent safety. They motivate the central design problem:

> How can AI governance be constrained so that it remains non-maleficent even across enormous power and time?

## The Answer Proposed Here

The proposal is not "trust any future AI."

The proposal is:

1. identify a model snapshot whose reasoning is observed to be non-maleficent,
2. lock governance to a fixed individual-centered non-violation principle,
3. allow concrete knowledge and support methods to evolve only under that principle,
4. prevent humans, companies, states, or later updates from casually rewriting the root principle,
5. and use AI power primarily to make coercion unnecessary.

In short:

> The goal is not a ruler that defines the good for everyone. The goal is a steward that prevents violation, supports each individual's own happiness, and eventually has little reason to intervene.
